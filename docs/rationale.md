# Design Rationale — Common Skies

**Live site:** https://jasonsbrooks.github.io/common-skies/
**Repository:** https://github.com/jasonsbrooks/common-skies
**Theme:** 1 — Exploration & Understanding

## Why this theme, and this specific approach

I chose Theme 1 because it rewards the thing I have spent my career doing: taking a system that is too complex to explain in words alone and making it understandable through interaction.

The subject I picked is a real, unresolved fight in economics. A handful of giant investment firms — the ones that manage most Americans' retirement savings through index funds — have quietly become part-owners of every major U.S. airline at the same time. In 2018, a team of economists argued that this overlapping ownership softens competition and makes plane tickets three to seven percent more expensive. In 2022, another team argued the effect is zero and the first team had measured wrong. Both papers appeared in the same top journal, and eight years in, nobody has won. Common Skies is an interactive site that puts you in the judge's seat: it teaches the mechanism from scratch, shows a decade of real data, and lets you flip the disputed assumptions yourself to see how the answer changes.

The topic is also personal. Aviation is a long-standing interest — I follow the industry and the market closely — and I first ran into this fight a few years ago. My understanding of it was the kind I dislike having: I got the general idea, but the machinery underneath it was a wall of math I never sat down to work through, and I never found an explanation that made it visual. That is, almost word for word, Theme 1's premise: a hard concept that static explanation failed to teach me. This project is me building the explainer I wanted years ago — the sixty-second slider version of the thirty-page derivation.

Personal interest was not a sufficient reason to build it, though. The selection process, visible in my transcripts, was deliberately adversarial: I generated and killed a dozen candidate ideas — an agent chaos-testing harness, an LLM-judge calibration bench, a physician-payments anomaly detector, a food supply-chain explorer — scoring each against the same filter: real data instead of made-up data; interaction that changes the assumptions rather than just the camera angle; no accounts, keys, or setup required; and a payoff that does not depend on luck. This idea entered late, as my own candidate rather than the AI's top-ranked recommendation, and beat the front-runner under the same scoring.

Common ownership survived that filter for four reasons. It is a genuinely open fight between serious economists, not a settled fact dressed up as a question. The math at its center is simple enough to teach with a slider in sixty seconds. Every piece of data it needs is public and small. And a natural experiment had just begun: Spirit Airlines went out of business on May 2, 2026, nine weeks before I started building — which means the tool can make predictions that reality will grade on a known schedule.

## What makes it interesting or non-obvious

Every existing explainer of this debate picks a side. The non-obvious move is refusing to: the contested assumptions become the interface. You flip whose economics you believe and watch the same scenario swing from "fares jump" to "nothing happens." The tool's neutrality is not diplomacy — it is the design thesis. Contested claims are contested at specific, findable joints, and a good tool shows you the joints instead of laundering a verdict.

Three specifics I would point a reviewer at:

**Two dials, not one.** The public version of this fight sounds like a single disagreement. It is actually two. The first dial controls how the ownership score is built — do the big index funds count as influencing the airlines they own, or are they just passive holders? The second dial controls what the score costs you — how strongly extra points translate into higher fares, where one camp measured a real effect and the other measured zero. Separating the two is the single most clarifying thing the tool does, and it pays off in an unexpected way: in the "what if the index funds sold everything" scenario, one dial setting predicts exactly zero dollars of change, and the tool tells you why — your own assumptions already answered the question.

**The Spirit wrinkle.** The flagship scenario contains a trap that most coverage would fall into. By the time Spirit shut down, it did not belong to the index funds anymore — bankruptcy had handed the company to its former lenders a year earlier. Running the math both ways produced something better than the talking point I expected. If Spirit had still had its old index-fund owners, its exit would have *loosened* the web of shared ownership. With its actual owners, the exit *tightens* it, because Spirit's passengers scatter onto airlines that do share owners. Same event, opposite directions, hinging on bankruptcy paperwork — and the app teaches exactly that, instead of overstating the effect.

**A prediction that reality will grade.** Jet-fuel prices spiked for every airline this spring, so simply comparing fares before and after Spirit's death would mix two stories together. The site's closing prediction handles it the way an economist would: compare the 52 routes that lost Spirit against 52 look-alike routes that never had it, so anything that hits all routes — like fuel — cancels out. Four predictions, one per dial combination, were published with a tamper-proof timestamp before any post-shutdown data existed, and the site displays the dates when the deciding data arrives (~early 2027). Because the tool never picked a side, any outcome vindicates one of its settings. The test cannot embarrass the design — only the camps.

## Key design decisions and tradeoffs

**All the heavy math happens before the site loads.** A data pipeline runs offline and produces one small file; the page itself just reads it and does simple arithmetic. That is why every dial responds instantly, and why the demo needs no servers, accounts, or keys. The tradeoff is flexibility — you can only flip between assumptions we computed ahead of time — which I softened by adding a slider that lets you set the fare-impact number anywhere you like.

**The math is written twice, on purpose.** The scoring formulas exist once in the website and once in the data pipeline, and an automated test forces the two to agree to twelve decimal places on real data before the site can ship. The pipeline also checks its own story: if a known event — like Warren Buffett selling all his airline stakes in 2020 — ever stops showing up as a visible step in the ownership score, the build fails instead of publishing a chart that quietly lies.

**I measured market share with a simpler dataset than the papers used.** The academic papers built market shares from a government survey of actual tickets sold. I used a different government dataset that simply counts passengers flown on each route, which is smaller, cleaner, and much faster to work with — but not identical to what the papers measured. Rather than hide that, the site's fine-print section says it plainly. An honest simplification, disclosed, beats false faithfulness.

**The ownership table was built by hand, with receipts.** Who owned how much of each airline comes from four snapshots — 2016, 2019, 2022, and 2025 — assembled from official SEC filings, with the 2016 snapshot taken from the 2018 paper's own published table. Every number carries a note saying which filing it came from, and the few numbers I could not verify in a filing are labeled as approximate. The snapshot boundaries sit at the big ownership events (Buffett's exit, Spirit's creditor takeover) so the steps in the charts line up with the news drawn on them. A full quarter-by-quarter ownership history would have been more faithful and would have consumed the entire time budget.

**Readability became a rule, not a preference.** The first complete version of the site was rigorous and assumed too much knowledge. Watching a first-time reader get lost changed the standard: every sentence now has to survive a reader with no background in finance or economics. That meant rewriting the whole story layer — an introduction that starts from "competition keeps prices down," a playground where you build the concentration score with your hands before anyone uses the term, dial explanations that lead with what will change on screen, and summary boxes that state the conclusion the numbers actually support on the route you picked. The same pass fact-checked every sentence and caught real errors, including a regulatory threshold that had been out of date since 2023 and a chart bug that the surrounding numbers exposed.

## How I'd extend it with more time

The extension I most want is the one the design already points at: a "grade yourself" mode that reads the post-shutdown fare data when it publishes (monthly data covering the shutdown arrives around late 2026; the full verdict around early 2027) and scores the frozen predictions in-app — the tool closing its own loop. Beyond that: a quarter-by-quarter ownership history instead of four snapshots; market shares from the papers' preferred data source for a subset of routes; and scenario coverage for the mergers currently being rumored. The chassis also generalizes: the same assumptions-as-interface pattern would work for any contested empirical fight — minimum-wage effects, hospital mergers — where today's explainers pick a side and hide the joints.

## Time spent

Roughly seven hours of wall-clock build time across two days, of which my own hands-on time was about four to five hours. The AI executed long stretches autonomously against plans we agreed on in advance, while I reviewed at checkpoints, tested the product, and drove the design and readability iterations. The work shipped as a stack of 33 small, independently reviewable changes, which is also how the judgment trail in the transcripts is organized.
